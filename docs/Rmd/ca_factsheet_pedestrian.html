Reported road casualties: Pedestrian factsheet

Author

Blaise Kelly

Published

March 4, 2026

This report is an adaption of the national DfT road safety factsheets, adapted for the Combined Authority (CA) region shown in the maps below. Combined Authority has been chosen as a significant proportion of the national report is based on modal data which is only available disagregated by CA.

1. Main findings

Between 2004 and 2024:

  • fatalities were down 75% from 8 to 2
  • serious injuries (adjusted) decreased by 44%
  • pedestrian traffic (distance walked) increased by 85%

Averaged over the period 2020 to 2024:

  • an average of 0.06 pedestrians died and 1.1 were seriously injured (adjusted) per week in reported road collisions
  • a majority of pedestrian fatalities (62%) do not occur at or within 20m of a junction compared to 51% of all seriously injured (adjusted) casualties
  • 50% of Pedestrian fatalities were in collisions involving a single car
  • 12% of pedestrian fatalities occurred on rural roads compared to 7% of all pedestrian casualties
  • 57% of pedestrian killed or seriously injured (KSI) (adjusted) casualties were male

The most common contributory factor allocated to pedestrians no contributory factors included in the public data

2. Pedestrian traffic and reported casualties

In 2024, 2 pedestrian were killed in West of England combined authority, whilst 70 were reported to be seriously injured and 162 slightly injured.

Table 1 and Chart 1 show that pedestrian traffic (measured by distance walked) has increased between 2004 and 2024 whilst fatalities, serious and slight injuries have fallen.

Between 2023 and 2024, pedestrian fatalities decreased by 60% while pedestrian traffic (distance walked) increased by 41%.

3. How far do Pedestrians travel?

The National Travel Survey (NTS) which provides the number of trips and average distance travelled (NTS0303_ca) by person per year for Combined Authority residents. This is used to derive casualty rates per mile traveled for pedestrian, which also use the landsat global 1km population data to estimate the population of the Combined Authority.

4. Casualty rates per mile travelled

The pedestrian casualty rate has increased for all severities in 2024 compared to 2004.

The overall casualty rate decreased by 73%. The fatality rate decreased by 87% compared to a 70% reduction for serious injuries and a 74% reduction for slight injuries.

5. Sex and age comparisons

Between 2020 and 2024, 57% of pedestrian casualties were male and 43% female.

There are 1.3 times more male than female pedestrian casualties overall. This compares to NA more for 30-39, NA more for 0-11and NA more for 70+ - the only age group in which female casualties outnumber males.

6. Which vehicles are involved in collisions with pedestrians?

Between 2020 and 2024, most pedestrian fatalities occurred in 1 vehicle collisions involving a car (8).

However, the highest proportion of casualties from single vehicle collisions involve 2 vehicles involved (5.5%). The second highest proportion (3.3%) occurred in collisions when 1 motorcycle.

7. Time of day of collisions

The weekday peak time for pedestrian KSIs is from 3pm to 8am. By contrast, the peak is from 2am to 7pm at weekends.

8. What type of road?

Chart 5 shows that between 2020 and 2024, 69% of pedestrian fatalities occurred on urban roads compared to 93% of all pedestrian casualties. 19% of pedestrian fatalities occurred on motorways. This would be people outside their vehicles whether they are moving at the time or not.

In this report, urban roads are defined as those within an area of population of 10,000 or more in England and Wales or more than 3,000 in Scotland - roads outside of these areas are classified as rural https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/road-length-statistics-information/road-lengths-in-great-britain-statistics-notes-and-definitions.
